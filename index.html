Information Society Discourses

Hover over each card to explore the main perspectives, characteristics, and impacts shaping our digital era.

Mercantilist

Mercantilist Discourse

  • The information society is a huge market with tremendous potential for economic growth supported by the use of digital technologies.
  • It is driven by the software, telecommunications, banking, and entertainment industries, representing the new economy.
  • Neoliberal economic principles argue that market rules are what should mainly guide and regulate the growth and development of the information society.
  • This perspective assumes that the development of the information society will mean greater material well-being and, consequently, more "happiness" for individuals.

Critical-Political

Critical-Political Discourse

  • Digital technologies must be at the service of social and human development, and not controlled by the interests of large industrial corporations in the capitalist world.
  • It seeks to build an alternative discourse to the mercantilist perspective, attempting to reorient technological development under social and political parameters.
  • It consists of the defense of civic and democratic rights for freedom of expression in the use of the Internet, standing against control attempts developed by different Western governments.

Technocentrist

Technocentrist Discourse

  • Digital technology is mythologized as the panacea of a more efficient society full of well-being for its citizens.
  • New technologies become the center of social and cultural change, maintaining that digital technologies are the axis of a revolutionary process of human civilization.
  • Cyberspace is an artificial world from which to build new experiential models of human communication and interaction.
  • This discourse acts as the current version of technological determinism.

Apocalyptic

Apocalyptic Discourse

  • Information and communication technologies represent the end of the ideals and values of modernity and the enlightened model of society.
  • This position questions the invasion of digital technologies over our civilization.
  • It argues that technology constitutes itself as the hegemonic ideology and, consequently, subordinates culture to technocratic interests.
